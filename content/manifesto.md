Fenris is a values-driven network. The work we do — product strategy, data governance, technical implementation — is inseparable from what we believe about data, power, and who gets to benefit from both.

This is our attempt to say that clearly. We invite you to read it in the same spirit it's written: as a working document, not a final word. We'll get some of it wrong. We expect to revise it.

---

We hold the following to be true, and to be important guiding ideas in how we engage and work with partners:

ONE **Data is public infrastructure, not private property.**

TWO **The communities that generate data should govern it.**

THREE **Sustainable data initiatives require shared ownership from the start.**

FOUR **Technical excellence is inseparable from ethical accountability.**

FIVE **The consultant's job is to make themselves unnecessary.**

## ONE Data is public infrastructure, not private property.

[Placeholder — write with the writing coach.]

Data about public health, civic participation, environmental conditions, and community welfare is not a byproduct of service delivery. It is a collective resource, generated by the actions and lives of people who rarely see any return on its value. When that data is locked inside proprietary systems, sold to the highest bidder, or simply neglected until it rots, something genuinely public is being privatized.

Infrastructure — roads, water systems, electrical grids — is understood to require public investment, public governance, and public accountability. Data that serves public purposes deserves the same treatment. The question is not whether to build data systems, but who they serve and who controls them.

## TWO The communities that generate data should govern it.

[Placeholder — write with the writing coach.]

There is a standard pattern in civic data work: a community generates data through its activities, needs, and interactions with institutions. An organization collects that data. A platform aggregates it. A funder decides what questions get asked of it. The community that generated it has no say in any of this.

This is not a neutral technical arrangement. It is a power arrangement. And it tends to reproduce the same asymmetries that the data was ostensibly collected to address.

Governance — who decides what data is collected, how it's used, who can access it, and when it's deleted — is not a compliance checkbox. It is the substance of the question: whose interests does this system actually serve?

## THREE Sustainable data initiatives require shared ownership from the start.

[Placeholder — write with the writing coach.]

Most data initiatives fail not because of bad technology but because of bad ownership structures. A vendor holds the keys. A single staff member holds the institutional knowledge. A funder holds the roadmap. When any of these relationships ends, the initiative ends with it.

Sustainability is designed in, not added later. It requires asking, from the first conversation: who will maintain this when we're gone? Who understands it well enough to change it? Who has the authority to make decisions about it?

These are not post-launch questions. They are the questions that determine whether the launch means anything.

## FOUR Technical excellence is inseparable from ethical accountability.

[Placeholder — write with the writing coach.]

There is a persistent fiction in technology work that technical decisions are neutral and ethical decisions are separate. The choice of data model is technical. The choice of what to measure is ethical. The choice of infrastructure is technical. The choice of who can access it is ethical.

This is a useful fiction for people who want to build things without being accountable for what they build. It is not a useful fiction for the people affected by those things.

Every technical decision embeds assumptions about who matters, what counts, and what is worth optimizing for. Making those assumptions explicit — and subjecting them to the same scrutiny as the code — is not a distraction from good technical work. It is part of what good technical work means.

## FIVE The consultant's job is to make themselves unnecessary.

[Placeholder — write with the writing coach.]

A consultant who is still needed in year three has either failed or is working against their client's interests. The goal of every engagement should be to build the capacity, knowledge, and confidence that makes continued external support optional.

This means sharing methods, not just delivering outputs. It means documenting decisions in ways that outlast the engagement. It means training the people who will maintain the work, not just the people who commissioned it. It means being honest when the answer to a problem is "you don't need us for this."

It also means being willing to say when an organization isn't ready for what they're asking for — and helping them get ready instead.
