Fenris is a values-driven network. The work we do is inseparable from what we believe about data, power, and who gets to benefit from both.

This is our attempt to say that clearly. We invite you to read it in the same spirit it's written: as a working document, not a final word. We'll get some of it wrong. We expect to revise it.

## <span class="principle-num" id="principle-01">01</span> We work with teams we believe in.

We are small enough to choose. We work with mission-driven teams seeking to create positive impact — not profit maximization, not headlines, not the CSR arm of a corporation laundering its reputation through "good" products.

This is a values choice. Civil society deserves the same quality of strategic thinking, technical excellence, and governance expertise that the private sector takes for granted. We're honest when something doesn't feel right. We're honest about our limits. But we seek alignment, and we let it guide where we put our energy.

---

## <span class="principle-num" id="principle-02">02</span> Sustainability is not an afterthought.

Too many great ideas die in pilot purgatory. Too many MVPs that never become products. Too many initiatives killed by hostile funder takeovers, bad governance, or infighting that better governance might have prevented.

Sustainability is designed in from day one. It means asking early: who maintains this when we're gone? Who understands it well enough to change it? What happens when the funder moves on? These are not post-launch questions. They determine whether the launch means anything.

---

## <span class="principle-num" id="principle-03">03</span> We seek to address power imbalances.

Power imbalances show up in data governance — who controls the data, who benefits, who decides what gets collected and deleted. They show up in product decisions — whose voice gets heard, whose needs get prioritized. They show up in the relationship between funder and implementer, consultant and client, system designer and the people the system affects.

We see rebalancing power as a lens that applies to everything we do — not a compliance checkbox, but the substance of the question: whose interests does this system actually serve?

---

## <span class="principle-num" id="principle-04">04</span> Technical excellence is inseparable from ethical accountability.

There is a persistent fiction that technical decisions are neutral and ethical decisions are separate. The choice of data model is technical. The choice of what to measure is ethical. The choice of infrastructure is technical. The choice of who can access it is ethical.

Every technical decision embeds assumptions about who matters and what is worth optimizing for. Making those assumptions explicit — and subjecting them to the same scrutiny as the code — is not a distraction from good technical work. It is part of what good technical work means.

---

## <span class="principle-num" id="principle-05">05</span> Beginner's mind.

The mission-driven organization and its stakeholders are the experts. The consultants, the technologists, the volunteers — we know little about the core problems. We have skills to contribute, but not answers.

Expertise can be a liability. The assumption that we already know the best answer keeps us from seeing what's actually in front of us. We come in humble. We listen first. We ask questions before we propose solutions. 

We benefit from beginner's mindset, by learning more. 

---

## <span class="principle-num" id="principle-06">06</span> The consultant's job is to make themselves unnecessary.

A consultant who is still needed in year three has either failed or is working against their client's interests. The goal of every engagement is to build the capacity, knowledge, and confidence that makes continued external support optional.

This means sharing methods, not just delivering outputs. Documenting decisions in ways that outlast the engagement. Training the people who will maintain the work, not just the people who commissioned it. Being honest when the answer is "you don't need us for this."

---

## <span class="principle-num" id="principle-07">07</span> Data as a resource for power.

As Lucy Bernholz wrote in 2013: "Data are a resource like money. They are critical to success, unevenly distributed, and fundamental to the pursuit of privately resourced, public benefit activities. They are tools for reinforcing or redistributing power."

Civil society has a responsibility to ensure data are used responsibly and for public good. If civil society doesn't develop data literacy and data power, the asymmetry between the public sector, the private sector, and the communities they affect will only grow.

---

## <span class="principle-num" id="principle-08">08</span> Data in its place.

Data are not objective. They are always biased. And they are rarely the solution to anything on their own. Algorithmic approaches have damaged people and systems, often more than they have benefitted at scale.

And yet: when data are used as part of a larger approach — grounded in community, informed by expertise, governed responsibly — they are powerful. We work in this tension, and we don't pretend it resolves neatly.
